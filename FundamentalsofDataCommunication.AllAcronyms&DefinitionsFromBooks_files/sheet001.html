Mesh Topology In a mesh topology, every device has a dedicated point-to-point link to every other device

link carries traffic only between the two devices it connects

ADVANTAGES
1. Guarantees that each connection can carry its own data load, thus eliminating the traffic problems that can occur when links must be shared by multiple devices
2. Robust. If one link becomes unusable, it does not incapacitate the entire system
3. Privacy or security
4. Point-to-point links make fault identification and fault isolation easy

DISADVANTAGES
1. every device must be connected to every other device, installation and reconnection are difficult
2. sheer bulk of the wiring can be greater
3. hardware required to connect each link is expensive
Star Topology each device has a dedicated point-to-point link only to a central controller, usually called a hub

not directly linked to one another

does not allow direct traffic between devices

ADVANTAGES
1. less expensive
2. each device needs only one link and one I/O port
3. easy to install and configure
4. less cabling
5. additions, moves, and deletions involve only one connection

DISADVANTAGES
1.  dependency of the whole topology on one single point
Bus Topology A bus topology, on the other hand, is multipoint
One long cable acts as a backbone
Nodes are connected to the bus cable by drop lines and taps
weaker and weaker as it travels farther

ADVANTAGES
1. ease of installation
2. can be laid along the most efficient path
3. redundancy is eliminated, backbone only stretches

DISADVANTAGES
1. bus is usually designed to be optimally efficient at installation
2. Signal reflection
3. Adding new devices may therefore require modification or replacement of the backbone
4.  fault or break in the bus cable stops all transmission
Ring Topology each device has a dedicated point-to-point connection with only the two devices on either side of it.A signal is passed along the ring in one direction, from device to device, until it reaches its destination. Each device in the ring incorporates a repeater.

ADVANTAGES
1. easy to install and configure
2. add or delete a device requires changing only two connections
3. only constraints are media and traffic consideration
4. fault isolation is simplified
5. signal is circulating at all time
6. alarm alerts the network operator to the problem and its location

DISADVANTAGES
1.  unidirectional traffic
2. a break in the ring (such as a disabled station) can disable the entire network

The Advanced Research Projects Agency (ARPA) in the Department of Defense (DoD) was interested in finding a way to connect computers so that the researchers they funded could share their findings, thereby reducing costs and eliminating duplication of effort.

In 1967 at an Association for Computing Machinery (ACM) meeting, ARPA presented its ideas for ARPANET, a small network of connected computers. The idea was that each host computer (not necessarily from the same manufacturer) would be attached to a specialized computer, called an interface message processor (IMP)
In 1969 ARPANET was a reality (internet was invented)
Four nodes, at the University of California at Los Angeles (UCLA), the University of California at Santa Barbara (UCSB), Stanford Research Institute (SRI), and the University of Utah, were connected via the IMPs to form a network. Software called the Network Control Protocol (NCP) provides communication between the hosts.
In 1972 Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn, both of whom were part of the core ARPANET group, collaborated on what they called the Internetting Project 1
In 1973 Cerf and Kahn landmark 1973 paper outlined the protocols to achieve end-to-end delivery of packets. This paper on Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) included concepts such as encapsulation, the datagram, and the functions of a gateway.
